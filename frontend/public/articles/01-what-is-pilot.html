How Pilot Works

Understanding how Pilot works is essential for anyone looking to try this exciting crash game. Here's a step-by-step breakdown of the gameplay:

Step 1: Place Your Bet

Before each round begins, you have a betting window to place your wager. You can typically place one or two bets simultaneously, allowing for different strategies within the same round.

Step 2: Watch the Takeoff

Once the betting window closes, the vintage plane begins its ascent. A multiplier starts at 1.00x and increases as the plane climbs higher into the sky. The longer the plane flies, the higher the multiplier grows.

Step 3: Decide When to Cash Out

This is where the excitement (and the skill) comes in. You must decide when to click the "Cash Out" button to secure your winnings. Your final payout is your bet amount multiplied by the current multiplier when you cash out.

Step 4: The Plane Flies Away

At a random point, the plane will fly away, ending the round. If you haven't cashed out by this time, you lose your entire bet. The timing of when the plane flies away is determined by a provably fair algorithm.

Example Round

You bet $10. The plane takes off and the multiplier climbs: 1.00x... 1.50x... 2.00x... 2.75x... You cash out at 2.75x and receive $27.50 (your $10 bet x 2.75). The plane continues to 4.32x before flying away. You secured a profit of $17.50!

Understanding Flight Types

The flight type system in Pilot adds an extra layer of interest to the game by categorizing rounds based on their outcome. While these categories don't affect your gameplay strategy (the outcome is always random), they provide a fun way to track round history and understand the distribution of results.

It's crucial to understand that the flight type of a round is determined after the round concludes. There is no way to predict what type of flight the next round will be. Each round is independent, and past results do not influence future outcomes - this is a fundamental principle known as statistical independence.

Important: Gambler's Fallacy Warning

Believing that a "Moon Flight" is "due" because there haven't been any recently is a classic example of the gambler's fallacy. Each round in Pilot is completely independent, and the probability of any given outcome remains constant regardless of previous results.

RTP and House Edge

Pilot's 97% RTP translates to a 3% house edge, which means the casino retains 3% of all wagers over time. While this is favorable compared to many casino games, it's essential to understand what this means for players:

  • RTP is calculated over millions of rounds, not individual sessions
  • Short-term results can vary significantly from the theoretical RTP
  • The house edge ensures the casino maintains a mathematical advantage
  • No strategy can overcome the house edge in the long run

Understanding these mathematical realities is crucial for maintaining healthy gambling habits and realistic expectations.
