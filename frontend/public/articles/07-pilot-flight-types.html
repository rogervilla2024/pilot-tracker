Introduction to Flight Types

One of Pilot's most distinctive features is its flight type classification system. Unlike other crash games that simply display the crash multiplier, Pilot categorizes each round into a thematic flight type based on how far the vintage plane travels before flying away.

This system adds a layer of engagement and helps players visualize the round history in memorable terms. Instead of remembering "that round crashed at 1.35x," you might recall "there were three Short flights in a row."

Critical Understanding

Flight types are determined after each round concludes. They have no predictive value. Each round is independent, and the flight type of previous rounds does not influence future outcomes in any way.

How Flight Types Work

When a round ends, the final multiplier (the point at which the plane flew away) determines the flight type. Higher multipliers correspond to "longer" journeys in the thematic framework. The categorization is purely descriptive - it describes what happened, not what will happen.

Understanding Short Flights

Short flights represent the most frequent outcome in Pilot. The plane takes off but quickly flies away, leaving little time for players to react. These rounds are essential to the game's mathematics - they create the losses that fund the occasional high multiplier rounds.

Why Short Flights Are Common

For Pilot to maintain its 97% RTP while offering multipliers up to 10,000x, the majority of rounds must end at low values. If every round reached high multipliers, the game would be unsustainable. Short flights are the mathematical counterbalance to rare Moon flights.

Short Flight Scenarios

  • 1.01x crash: Nearly instant - almost no one can manually cash out in time
  • 1.20x crash: Very quick - only those with low auto cash outs profit
  • 1.45x crash: Brief but allows some reaction time

Understanding Domestic Flights

Domestic flights represent a middle ground - not as disappointing as Short flights, but not particularly remarkable either. These rounds give players reasonable time to decide whether to cash out and offer modest but achievable multipliers.

Strategic Considerations

Many conservative strategies target the Domestic flight range (particularly 1.50x - 2.00x). At these multipliers:

  • Win frequency is still relatively high
  • Payouts are meaningful (50-100%+ profit)
  • The risk/reward balance is moderate

This tension is the core of crash game psychology.

Understanding Intercontinental Flights

Intercontinental flights are notable events. When the multiplier breaks into double digits, the round becomes memorable. These flights can turn small bets into significant wins - a $10 bet cashed at 50x yields $500.

The Rarity Factor

Reaching 10x or higher requires both:

  • The round itself to reach that multiplier (random)
  • The player to hold that long (discipline/risk tolerance)

Many players cash out well before 10x, so even when these rounds occur, few fully capitalize on them.

Understanding Moon Flights

Moon flights are the legendary rounds that players dream about. When a round exceeds 100x, it enters exceptional territory. The maximum multiplier of 10,000x represents the ultimate Moon flight - turning $10 into $100,000.

The Reality of Moon Flights

While exciting to imagine, it's important to understand:

  • Extremely rare: 100x+ happens roughly once per 100 rounds on average
  • 1000x+ is rarer still: Perhaps once per 1,000 rounds
  • 10,000x is exceptional: You might never see one in your playing career
  • Most players cash out earlier: Even during Moon flights, most have already cashed

Moon Flight Scenarios

  • 150x crash: Rare and exciting - $10 becomes $1,500
  • 500x crash: Very rare - major win for any player still in
  • 2,500x crash: Exceptional - $10 becomes $25,000
  • 10,000x (max): Legendary - maximum possible win

Moon Flight Reality Check

Don't structure your gambling around waiting for Moon flights. They're too rare to rely on, and the losses accumulated while waiting will typically exceed any eventual Moon flight win. The house edge applies equally to all strategies.

Why Flight Types Cannot Be Predicted

It's crucial to understand why observing flight type patterns cannot help you win:

Random Number Generation

Each round's crash point is determined by a Random Number Generator (RNG) that produces outcomes with no pattern or memory. The algorithm doesn't know or care about previous results.

Statistical Independence

Each round is statistically independent. This means:

  • The probability of any outcome is the same every round
  • Previous results do not influence future results
  • Streaks of Short flights don't make Moon flights more likely
  • A Moon flight doesn't make subsequent Short flights more likely

The Gambler's Fallacy

Believing that a Moon flight is "due" after many Short flights is the gambler's fallacy. Common misconceptions include:

  • "It's been 50 rounds since a Moon flight, one must be coming" - False. The probability remains constant.
  • "Three Short flights in a row means the next will be longer" - False. No relationship exists.
  • "The game balances out over time, so good flights are coming" - Partially true statistically, but not in the way that helps you. Regression to mean occurs through new average results, not compensating outcomes.

Mathematical Truth

If Short flights have ~35% probability, that probability remains ~35% regardless of whether the last 10 rounds were Short flights or Moon flights. The dice don't remember previous rolls.

Tracking Flight History

While tracking flight history cannot predict future results, it can still serve some purposes:

Legitimate Uses of History Tracking

  • Entertainment: Adds engagement and interest to the game
  • Understanding the game: Helps visualize typical distributions
  • Personal records: Track your own wins and losses
  • Educational: Learn about probability through observation
  • Realistic expectations: See how often different flight types actually occur

Illegitimate Uses (Don't Do This)

  • Using patterns to predict next round's outcome
  • Increasing bets because a certain flight type is "due"
  • Developing "systems" based on observed sequences
  • Believing you've found exploitable patterns

Frequently Asked Questions

Are flight types the same across all casinos?

The core categorization system is part of Pilot's design by Gamzix, so it should be consistent. However, specific multiplier thresholds for each category may have minor variations.

Do flight types affect payouts?

No. Your payout is based solely on the multiplier at which you cash out, not the flight type classification. Flight types are purely descriptive.

Can I filter or search for specific flight types in history?

This depends on the interface. Some versions of Pilot may allow filtering round history by flight type, which can be interesting for observation but not for prediction.

I've seen 10 Short flights in a row. Is a Moon flight coming?

No. Each round is independent. The probability of a Moon flight (roughly 1%) is the same whether the previous round was a Short flight or another Moon flight.

Should I bet more when I think a Moon flight is coming?

No. You cannot predict when Moon flights will occur. Betting more based on expected outcomes that you cannot predict is a losing strategy.

What's the longest streak of Short flights possible?

Theoretically, there's no limit. With ~35% probability per round, long streaks are unlikely but statistically possible. Don't let streaks influence your betting - they're normal variance.

  • Past results don't influence future outcomes
  • No pattern in flight history gives you an edge
  • The house edge applies equally to all rounds
